# Design spec: "It Never Knows That It Doesn't Know"

## One-sentence pitch

The third foundations post: hallucination is not a separate broken mode, it is the next-token machine running where its support is thin, and that reframing tells you where the model fails and what to do about it.

## Goals

- Explain hallucination from the mechanism, not as a mysterious defect. Same engine, weaker ground.
- Build on the token post (odds, not facts) and the loop post (plausible next token). Stand alone, link back optionally.
- Cash it out in three builder payoffs: confidence is not a signal, where it fails is predictable, verification goes outside the model.
- Leave the reader able to forecast where to distrust the model and where to put the check.

## Non-goals (load-bearing)

- No taxonomy of hallucination types.
- No training-process deep dive.
- No RAG-architecture tutorial. Retrieval appears as "stacking the odds," one aside, not a how-to.
- No benchmark or eval methodology.
- No claim hallucination is fully solvable. Not a doom take, not a hype take.

## The angle

Same series spine: mechanics as leverage. Hallucination is the same machine from the last two posts, predicting a plausible next token, running where training gave it thin support. There is no separate bug to patch. Understanding that tells you where it breaks and what to do.

## Hook

The flat statement. There is no moment inside the model where it knows it does not know. The same machine that gives a correct answer gives a confident wrong one, and from the inside the two are identical.

## Framing

Same engine, weaker ground. Strong, repeated training support makes the plausible token usually true. Thin support still yields a plausible token, because that is all the model ever produces. That plausible-but-unsupported token is the hallucination.

## Proposed structure

1. **Hook.** No internal "I don't know." Same machine, same confidence, both outcomes.
2. **Why it happens.** One-line callback: predicts a plausible next token from odds, no fact store, compressed patterns from training. Strong support, the plausible token is true. Thin support, still a plausible token. That is the hallucination. Same process, weaker ground.
3. **Confidence is not a signal (payoff A).** The fluent tone is generated by the same loop as the facts, so smoothness says nothing about truth. "Are you sure?" just generates a plausible answer to that question. The doubt was never represented, so you cannot get it from inside.
4. **Where it's worst is predictable (payoff B).** Thin support is forecastable: rare facts, exact numbers, names, citations, recent events, anything specific and uncommon. Common repeated knowledge is strong ground. The more precise and obscure the ask, the weaker the footing.
5. **Verification goes outside the model (payoff C).** No prompting your way out. Ground the loop in something real (retrieval, sources, tools) and check output against ground truth. Aside: putting the real fact in context makes the true token high-probability, so retrieval stacks the odds, not magic. The check lives where truth lives, outside.
6. **Close.** The model gives you plausible, always. Truth is yours to supply or verify. Tie back: the loop runs on odds, and odds are not facts.
